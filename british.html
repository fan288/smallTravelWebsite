Location

The United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland, commonly known as the United Kingdom (UK) or Britain,[m] is a country in Northwestern Europe, off the coast of the continental mainland. It comprises England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. The UK includes the island of Great Britain, the north-eastern part of the island of Ireland, and most of the smaller islands within the British Isles, covering 94,354 square miles (244,376 km2). Northern Ireland shares a land border with the Republic of Ireland; otherwise, the UK is surrounded by the Atlantic Ocean, the North Sea, the English Channel, the Celtic Sea and the Irish Sea. It maintains sovereignty over the British Overseas Territories, which are located across various oceans and seas globally.

History

British history is a complex narrative shaped by invasions, colonization, and significant social changes. The story begins with prehistoric settlements, followed by the Roman conquest in 43 AD, which introduced infrastructure and urbanization. The fall of the Roman Empire led to the Anglo-Saxon period, where various kingdoms emerged and Christianity spread. The Norman Conquest in 1066, marked by William the Conqueror's victory at the Battle of Hastings, dramatically altered the political landscape and introduced feudalism, laying the foundations for modern governance.

The subsequent centuries saw the rise of the monarchy, with pivotal events such as the signing of the Magna Carta in 1215, which established principles of legal rights and limited royal power. The Tudor period, particularly under Henry VIII and Elizabeth I, was marked by religious upheaval and exploration, contributing to England's cultural and political identity. The English Civil War in the 17th century challenged the monarchy's authority, leading to the temporary establishment of a republic and eventually shaping the constitutional monarchy that exists today.

The 19th and early 20th centuries were transformative for Britain, marked by the expansion of the British Empire, which became the largest empire in history. This era saw Britain at the forefront of global trade, politics, and cultural exchange, influencing many regions worldwide. However, the empire's expansion often involved significant exploitation and conflict. The two World Wars brought immense social and economic challenges, reshaping British society, while post-war developments led to decolonization and a reassessment of national identity.

Culture

British culture is a rich amalgamation of traditions, literature, music, and art that has evolved over centuries. Literature has played a pivotal role, with iconic figures such as William Shakespeare, Jane Austen, and Charles Dickens profoundly influencing English literature and global storytelling. The British are known for their contributions to theater, with London's West End being a major hub for world-class performances, showcasing everything from classic plays to contemporary musicals.

Culinary traditions in Britain have also evolved, characterized by hearty dishes and regional specialties. Classics like fish and chips, shepherd's pie, and Sunday roasts are staples of British cuisine, often enjoyed in the warm ambiance of traditional pubs. The British pub culture serves as a social cornerstone, where communities gather to enjoy food, drink, and conversation. Additionally, the rise of multiculturalism in recent decades has enriched British cuisine, introducing diverse flavors from around the world.

In addition to its literary and musical heritage, British culture includes a diverse range of festivals and traditions that celebrate its rich history. Events like Notting Hill Carnival and Glastonbury Festival showcase the vibrancy of contemporary British life, blending music, art, and multicultural influences. The British royal family also plays a significant role in cultural identity, with events such as royal weddings and the Changing of the Guard attracting global attention. British humor, often characterized by its dry wit and self-deprecation, has left a lasting impact on comedy, influencing television shows and stand-up performances worldwide. This blend of tradition and modernity continues to define British culture, making it a dynamic and evolving entity.

Attraction

The British Museum

The British Museum was founded in 1753 and opened its doors in 1759. It was the first national museum to cover all fields of human knowledge, open to visitors from across the world. Enlightenment ideals and values – critical scrutiny of all assumptions, open debate, scientific research, progress and tolerance – have marked the Museum since its foundation. The Museum is driven by an insatiable curiosity for the world, a deep belief in objects as reliable witnesses and documents of human history, sound research, as well as the desire to expand and share knowledge.

Elgin Marbles

Among the British Museum’s most famous holdings are the Elgin Marbles, which were removed in the early 19th century from the Parthenon in Athens and shipped to England by arrangement of Thomas Bruce, 7th Lord Elgin. The Greek government frequently demanded the return of the marbles, but the British Museum—claiming among other reasons that it had saved the marbles from certain damage and deterioration—did not accede, and the issue remained controversial.

The Rosetta Stone

Other objects in the collection include Greek sculptures from the Mausoleum of Halicarnassus and from the Temple of Artemis at Ephesus; the Rosetta Stone, which provided the key to reading ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs; the Black Obelisk and other Assyrian relics from the palace and temples at Calah (modern Nimrūd) and Nineveh; exquisite gold, silver, and shell work from the ancient Mesopotamian city of Ur; the so-called Portland Vase, a 1st-century-ce cameo glass vessel found near Rome; treasure from the 7th-century-ce ship burial found at Sutton Hoo, Suffolk; and Chinese ceramics from the Ming and other dynasties.

Getting There and Around

You are advised to book a free ticket (Opens in new window) in advance of your visit. Entry to the Museum is via the Main entrance on Great Russell Street or the Montague Place entrance. Please note walk-up entry will only be possible at the Montague Place entrance, and entry is dependent on capacity.

Getting to the British Museum by train is easy with Avanti. The nearest train station is London Euston, which Avanti trains travel to every day. You can walk to the British Museum from London Euston in 14 minutes by following these directions:

Leave the station and turn left onto Euston Road. Then, take the left at the crossroads onto Gower Street. Finally, take the left at Montague Place, which is directly beyond Malet Street Gardens.

You’ll see plenty of signposts to help you along your way. You won’t miss the Museum, it surrounds the largest covered courtyard in Europe. The Museum’s roof features 1,656 individually shaped glass panels, which sit in a steel frame. And in true Greek Revival style, the front of the Museum boasts 44 45-foot columns.

The British Museum’s address is Great Russell Street, WC1B 3DG. There is also a second entrance at Montague Place, WC1E 7JW.
