South Korea

Location

South Korea, officially known as the Republic of Korea, is located in East Asia on the southern part of the Korean Peninsula. It is bordered by North Korea to the north, while to the south, it faces the Yellow Sea. The country is flanked by the Korea Strait to the east and the Yellow Sea to the west. South Korea shares maritime borders with Japan to the southeast, making it a significant hub in the region. Its geographical position has contributed to its rich cultural heritage and economic development, as it serves as a crossroads between various Asian nations.

History

South Korea's history is marked by a rich tapestry of dynasties, invasions, and cultural evolution. The earliest known kingdom, Gojoseon, emerged around 2333 BCE. Over the centuries, various dynasties, such as the Silla, Goryeo, and Joseon, played significant roles in shaping the region's identity. The Joseon Dynasty, which lasted for over five centuries, is particularly notable for its advancements in Confucianism, science, and the arts. The invention of the Korean script, Hangul, in the 15th century by King Sejong the Great is a pivotal moment in Korean history, promoting literacy and cultural cohesion.

The 20th century brought profound changes, including Japanese colonization from 1910 to 1945, which significantly impacted Korean society and culture. Following Japan's defeat in World War II, Korea was divided into two separate states—the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (North Korea) and the Republic of Korea (South Korea). The Korean War (1950-1953) solidified this division, leading to ongoing political tensions and differing paths of development, with South Korea emerging as a democratic and economically prosperous nation.

Culture

South Korean culture is a vibrant blend of traditional and modern influences, characterized by its unique customs, cuisine, and artistic expressions. Traditional elements, such as hanbok (traditional clothing), tea ceremonies, and folk music, coexist with contemporary trends like K-pop, fashion, and digital media. The global phenomenon of K-pop has not only transformed the music industry but also influenced fashion and youth culture worldwide, with groups like BTS and BLACKPINK leading the charge.

Cuisine is another vital aspect of South Korean culture, renowned for its bold flavors and diverse dishes. Staples like kimchi, a fermented vegetable dish, and bibimbap, a mixed rice dish, showcase the emphasis on fresh ingredients and communal dining. Festivals, such as Chuseok (harvest festival) and Seollal (Lunar New Year), highlight the importance of family and community, featuring traditional games, food, and rituals that honor ancestors. This rich cultural heritage continues to evolve, reflecting South Korea's dynamic society.

Attraction

N Seoul Tower

N Seoul Tower The N Seoul Tower (Korean: N 서울타워), officially the YTN Seoul Tower and a.k.a. the Namsan Tower, is a communication and observation tower located on the summit of Namsan (262 m (860 ft)) in central Seoul, South Korea. The 236-metre-tall (774 ft) structure marks the second highest point in Seoul and is considered a local landmark.

Built in 1971, N Seoul Tower is South Korea's first general radio wave tower, providing TV and radio broadcasting in Seoul. Currently, the tower broadcasts signals for Korean media outlets, such as KBS, MBC and SBS.

N Seoul Tower Observatory

The main draw of N Seoul Tower is its observatory. Observe the city below from the height of the clouds by making your way up to the tower’s observation deck. The main observation deck on 3F features large, floor-to-ceiling windows the whole way around the tower, allowing you to experience the full 360-degree views. There is information on various points of interests that can be seen through the windows and plenty of comfy chairs around as well. One floor up from here, on 4F, you’ll find the digital observatory. This floor features high-powered telescopes and each window panel lets you know which major world city you are facing.

The N Seoul Tower observatory is open year-round from 10 a.m. to 11 pm Sunday to Friday and from 10 a.m. to midnight on Saturdays. However, entry up to the observatory closes half an hour before closing time, so make sure you arrive before then. Access to the observatory also requires an admission ticket. The price of admission is 10,000 won for visitors 13 years of age and 8,000 won for children between three and 12 and seniors over 65.

N Seoul Tower's Hanbok Culture Experience Center

The Hanbok Culture Experience Center is located in the lobby of the N Seoul Tower plaza. It offers visitors a chance to take part in two traditional cultural experiences themed around the Joseon Dynasty. In the Hanbok Experience, guests get to dress up and accessorize in traditional attire and take pictures in the photo zones. In the Traditional Wedding Experience, guests dress up in wedding attire and have their photos taken by a professional photographer. The center is open from 11 a.m. to 10 p.m. Tickets cost 4,000 won for guests 14 years and older and 1,000 won for children under 13. Children under 7 years of age enjoy free admission. Note that the price of admission does not include Hanbok rental. It is also an extra charge to take part in the Hanbok Experience and the Traditional Wedding Experience.

How to Get to N Seoul Tower

There are a number of ways to get to N Seoul Tower depending on how you prefer to get around on your travels. While it is possible to walk up Namsan Mountain and reach the tower, the trails are steep and fairly difficult. The most recommended transportation options for tourists are the Namsan Cable Car, a shuttle bus, or a city tour bus.
